Project Highlights

Cultivating Change: The Story of Team Flow Chain and the Newark Bodega Project

In the bustling heart of Newark, a determined group of Bergen Community College students stood at the threshold of a great, but worth-while challenge. The initiative began when Dr. Kevin Lyons, Associate Professor at Rutgers Business School–Newark, envisioned a project that would develop sustainable urban food logistics by partnering directly with local communities. To turn this vision into reality, Dr. Lyons reached out to Dr. Edward Sanchez at Bergen Community College, entrusting him with forming a capable and passionate student team to lead the Newark Bodega Project.

Building the Team

Dr. Sanchez first connected with Kevin Kim, a dedicated student leader and active member of Bergen's 3SP Club, recognizing Kevin's strong community ties and commitment to social impact. Kevin enthusiastically partnered with Dr. Sanchez, providing valuable insights and leveraging his extensive social network to supply essential contacts within Newark's bodega community.

Recognizing the project's need for robust data analysis and technical oversight, Dr. Sanchez recruited Jensy Jimenez, who had recently completed the Data Analytics and Dashboard Program at Bergen's Continuing Education Center, under the mentorship of Linda Caruso, Morgan Mulhall, and Bret Babish. Jensy, previously a Peer Leader with Bergen's Title V Program, humbly stepped into the role of Data Analytics Administrator, eager to contribute and learn alongside his teammates.

Dr. Sanchez then carefully selected additional team members whose complementary skills would enhance the project's success: Estrella Luna (Plant Biology and Data Collection), Shanandra Ubiera (Community Communications), Michael la Torre (Business Development and Community Outreach), Riley O'Sullivan (Marketing and Social Media), and Yulissa Aguila (Computer Science and Digital Communication). In preparation, the team met regularly under Dr. Sanchez's guidance, solidifying their roles, developing strategies, and establishing a collaborative team dynamic long before the official project start date of June 30, 2025.

Early Days and Hardships

From the outset, Team Flow Chain encountered the complexity of connecting scattered community gardens and small, independently operated bodegas into a coherent supply network. Jensy supported this effort by creating a modest digital platform, known as the Bodega Project Website, designed to organize resources and visually map potential partnerships.

However, initial visits to Newark's bodegas quickly revealed substantial challenges. Store owners were frequently unavailable, staff were overwhelmed with daily operations, and language barriers posed continuous communication hurdles. Shanandra Ubiera, as the Communications Lead and Translator, understood firsthand that overcoming these barriers required far more than translation—it demanded patience, empathy, and genuine relationship-building.

Meanwhile, Kevin Kim, serving as Bodega Supply Chain Specialist, identified logistical inefficiencies limiting fresh produce availability. Many local farms struggled to supply enough products, and urban gardens were often neglected or inaccessible.

As the Plant Biology Expert, Estrella Luna faced difficulties assessing viable urban plots, frequently finding neglected gardens overtaken by weeds or replaced by urban development. Each discovery brought more challenges than solutions.

On the business front, Michael la Torre found that many bodega owners were uninterested or lacked resources to measure key performance indicators (KPIs), complicating efforts to demonstrate the project's long-term viability.

Turning Points and Innovative Solutions

Moving forward, Team Flow Chain plans to leverage guidance from mentors at Rutgers Newark and Bergen Community College, working collaboratively toward achievable and impactful solutions.

Jensy Jimenez, utilizing his data analytics background, aims to develop accessible dashboards and digital tools to help the team visualize and coordinate potential partnerships between local farms, gardens, and bodegas. These interactive resources have the potential to streamline communication and logistics significantly, making the project's objectives clearer and more attainable.

Shanandra Ubiera will lead culturally inclusive community engagement workshops, patiently building trust through empathy and authentic dialogue. Her approach aims to gradually transform hesitant community members into active participants, strengthening connections between bodegas and community gardeners.

To address local food production limitations, Estrella Luna intends to introduce sustainable agricultural practices such as hydroponics and vertical farming. Her research-based methods and enthusiasm for plant biology position her to effectively revive abandoned gardens, enhancing crop yields and making consistent local sourcing a realistic goal.

Drawing from his strong understanding of logistics, Kevin Kim plans to optimize inventory management and distribution networks. Kevin's practical approach to addressing supply chain inefficiencies is expected to reduce product spoilage, lower lead times, and improve the freshness of produce at local bodegas.

Michael la Torre aims to educate bodega owners about the benefits of tracking key performance indicators (KPIs), providing them with accessible tools to monitor performance effectively. His efforts intend to gradually encourage businesses to embrace data-informed decisions, leading to improved operations and profitability.

Riley O'Sullivan will support these initiatives through targeted marketing strategies and dynamic social media campaigns designed to highlight community efforts, attract local engagement, and raise awareness about the project's goals and milestones.

Behind the scenes, Yulissa Aguila will manage digital communication, ensuring technology operates smoothly and stakeholders stay continuously informed. Her skillset promises to enhance the team's online presence, strengthen communication channels, and promote transparency.

Together, Team Flow Chain is positioned to turn these ambitious yet practical ideas into meaningful realities. With dedicated mentorship, ongoing support, and each member's unique expertise, the path ahead promises innovation, growth, and lasting community impact.

A Vision for the Future

Team Flow Chain learned through experience that lasting change requires resilience, adaptability, and sincere community relationships. Their initial struggles became the catalyst for creativity, innovation, and lasting collaboration, laying a firm foundation for sustainable urban food supply chains.

As the project moves forward, Team Flow Chain hopes to expand the Newark model beyond city limits, potentially reaching Bergen County bodegas and other communities across New Jersey. Future initiatives include deeper integration with Newark's "Adopt-a-Lot" program, broadening the scope of urban vertical farming, and ongoing community education around food sustainability.

Together, Jensy, Kevin, Estrella, Shanandra, Michael, Riley, and Yulissa embody the values of innovation, dedication, and teamwork. Guided by Dr. Kevin Lyons' vision and Dr. Edward Sanchez's steady leadership, their journey demonstrates the transformative potential of collective action, humility, and unwavering commitment to healthier communities—one bodega, one garden, and one neighborhood at a time.

About the Project

Building on Team Flow Chain’s groundbreaking work, this community-driven initiative aims to create a resilient, sustainable food supply chain linking local farms and school gardens directly to Newark’s bodegas. At the heart of this model is the belief that food access is a fundamental right for everyone.

Supported by Bergen Community College, a federally designated Hispanic-Serving Institution, and Rutgers Business School–Newark, a leading hub for urban economic development and innovation, this project is uniquely positioned to make meaningful, lasting change. The project originated under the visionary leadership of Dr. Kevin Lyons, Associate Professor at Rutgers Business School, whose commitment to sustainable urban logistics inspired this impactful partnership.

Generously funded by the USDA and NIFA, under the leadership and coordination of Dr. Edward Sanchez, this initiative emphasizes experiential learning and leadership training in key areas such as agriculture, business, food systems, and environmental science, empowering students and communities alike.

Mission & Objectives

Mission

Empower Bergen students through practical training and real-world experiences, equipping them with essential skills to design, implement, and maintain sustainable urban food supply chains.

Objectives

  • Develop a viable, replicable prototype supply chain model connecting local farms and community gardens with Newark bodegas.
  • Foster ongoing research and innovation in sustainable urban logistics and agriculture.
  • Strengthen community relationships and enhance local food security through education, collaboration, and resource sharing.
Program Dates: June 30, 2025 – July 24, 2025

Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

Team Flow Chain's experience highlighted the critical importance of tracking key performance metrics. The following indicators help evaluate and ensure ongoing success and sustainability:

  • Inventory Shortage: Minimizing losses due to theft, damage, fraud, or miscounts.
  • Supplier Lead Time: Reducing the duration from placing orders to product shipment.
  • Warehouse Utilization: Optimizing the ratio of used to available warehouse space.
  • Cold Chain Compliance: Ensuring safe and reliable temperature control throughout transportation.
  • Delivery Time Variability: Narrowing gaps between expected and actual delivery times.
  • Delivery Accuracy: Maximizing the percentage of on-time, undamaged deliveries.

Urban Agriculture: Indoor Vertical Farming

Inspired by Team Flow Chain’s innovative solutions, and supported by insights from Dr. Lyons and Dr. Sanchez, this initiative incorporates indoor vertical farming, a pioneering practice transforming urban agriculture:

  • Year-Round Production: Enables continuous farming cycles independent of weather and seasons.
  • Local Food Security: Strengthens food systems by significantly reducing transportation emissions and reliance on distant suppliers.
  • Controlled Environments: Efficiently manages essential growth factors—such as light, water, and nutrients—to optimize plant yields.
  • Reduced Pest Issues: Minimizes pest-related losses, thereby increasing crop consistency and quality.
  • Frequent Harvests: Supports multiple harvests per year, greatly enhancing local food availability and freshness.

Through the combined leadership of Dr. Kevin Lyons and Dr. Edward Sanchez, the project integrates academic expertise with practical innovation, creating a robust and sustainable urban food network to ensure communities enjoy consistent access to fresh, nutritious produce.
